## What

Choose where to submit before you write the introduction. Your target journal shapes the abstract limit, the reference style, the IMRaD vs. registered-report structure, and what your reader expects to take away.

## When

As soon as the analysis is locked. Re-evaluate after major revisions if scope drifts.

## How

1. List 4-6 candidate journals where similar studies have appeared in the last 24 months.
2. Score each on: scope fit, audience match, indexed in PubMed/Medline/Embase, open-access policy, APC cost, average time-to-first-decision, acceptance rate, and reporting-guideline alignment.
3. Run a one-author + one-mentor "sanity check" — predicted desk-reject risk vs. peer-review risk.
4. Use a journal-finder tool only as a corroborator (Jane, JournalGuide, Edanz, the Elsevier/Springer/Wiley finders); never let one decide for you.

## Pitfalls

- Impact factor worship. IF is one signal; readership and time-to-decision often matter more.
- Predatory journals. Red flags: aggressive email solicitation, broad/generic scope ("any biomedical topic"), unverifiable editorial board, no DOI, fees disclosed only on acceptance, "Beall-style" list presence, no PubMed indexing, fake metrics ("Universal Impact Factor"). Check Think-Check-Submit and DOAJ.
- Mismatched format. A 1,200-word case-report journal will desk-reject a 5,000-word RCT.
- Slow journals on a clinical timely topic. Check Clarivate/Scimago "average time to first decision" or PubsHub.

## Template

A 3-row short-list table for your supervisor:

```
Journal | Scope fit (1-5) | Avg time-to-decision | Open access | Reason if rejected, next stop
```
