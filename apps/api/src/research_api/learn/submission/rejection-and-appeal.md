## What

Most submitted manuscripts are rejected — desk rejection (no peer review) or after-review rejection. You have two options: pivot to a different journal, or appeal.

## When to pivot vs appeal

Pivot (and re-submit elsewhere) when:

- The decision is desk rejection on grounds of scope/fit.
- The reviews identify real, fixable methodological weaknesses.
- The decision is "rejection with the door open" / "rejection but the editor invites a substantially revised submission as a new manuscript".
- You suspect appealing would burn the editorial relationship for marginal gain.

Appeal when:

- A reviewer made a clear factual error (misread the methods, misattributed a citation).
- A reviewer raised a concern out of scope for the manuscript type.
- The decision rests on a misinterpretation that you can substantiate with one or two paragraphs.
- The editor's reasoning contradicts a clearly satisfied requirement.

## How — appeal

1. Wait 48-72 hours so you write from analysis rather than annoyance.
2. Write a calm, evidence-based letter to the editor (often the EiC or appeals editor). State which decision you appeal, summarise the factual disagreement in 1-2 paragraphs per point, and request specific action (re-review with a different reviewer, re-evaluation by a section editor).
3. Do not re-litigate the entire review. Cherry-pick the strongest 1-2 points.
4. Accept the appeal outcome — appealing twice on the same decision is poor form.

## How — pivot

1. Within a week, regroup with co-authors.
2. Update the manuscript using the reviewers' constructive comments — even if you disagree, addressing them now strengthens the resubmission.
3. Re-evaluate journal fit (see [picking-a-journal](?slug=picking-a-journal)). A slightly lower-ranked journal with stronger scope alignment is often the right next step.
4. Write a fresh cover letter for the new journal. Disclose the previous review history if the new journal participates in a manuscript-transfer network (BMJ, Nature Portfolio, PLOS, Elsevier transfer-of-review).
5. Update preprint with a new version if changes are substantive.

## Pitfalls

- Submitting the same manuscript unchanged to a sister journal — editors talk, and reviewer assignments often overlap.
- Appealing on the strength of "novelty" alone (rarely persuasive).
- Burning bridges. Editorial communities are small; a hostile appeal letter is remembered.

## Template (appeal)

```
Dear Dr. [Editor],

Thank you for your decision of [date] regarding [Manuscript ID].
We respectfully appeal this decision because of two factual issues
that we believe materially affected the assessment:

1. [Reviewer X comment quoted verbatim]. The manuscript does in fact
   address this in [Section, page, lines], where we [...].
2. [Reviewer Y comment quoted]. This concern relates to a study
   type other than the one we report (we present a [X], not a [Y]).

We would be grateful if you would consider re-reviewing under
[specific editorial action requested]. We accept that the final
decision rests with you.

Sincerely,
[Corresponding author]
```
