## Definition

Cost-utility analysis (CUA) is a specific form of cost-effectiveness analysis in which the clinical benefit is measured in QALYs (or, less commonly, DALYs averted). Because QALYs capture both length and quality of life and are common across diseases, CUA is the form most reimbursement agencies (NICE, CADTH, PBAC) prefer and the form that supports cross-disease comparisons.

## Interpretation

The headline output of a CUA is a cost-per-QALY ICER. CUA is appropriate whenever (a) quality of life is materially affected, (b) the intervention extends life, or (c) you need to compare across diseases. It is less informative when the only outcome that matters is mortality.

## Worked example (surgery)

Spinal fusion vs. non-operative care for chronic low-back pain. Surgery costs £14,200 more and gains 0.78 QALYs over 10 years. ICER = £18,205/QALY — under NICE's £20,000 threshold.

## How to report

Distinguish CUA from cost-effectiveness analysis explicitly in the abstract. State the utility instrument and tariff (e.g. EQ-5D-5L cross-walked to 3L UK values), the source of life expectancy, and the discount rate for both costs and effects (NICE default 3.5%; some agencies use 1.5% for effects).
