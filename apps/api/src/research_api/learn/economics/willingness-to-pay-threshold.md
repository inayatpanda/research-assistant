## Definition

A willingness-to-pay (WTP) threshold is the maximum a healthcare system is willing to pay for one additional QALY. Interventions with an ICER below the threshold are considered cost-effective and recommended for funding; those above are not.

## Common thresholds

- NICE (UK): £20,000-£30,000 per QALY for most appraisals; £100,000-£300,000 for highly specialised technologies (rare diseases).
- US (ICER and various payers): commonly cited bands of $50,000-$100,000 and $100,000-$150,000 per QALY; some analyses use $50k as a lower bound and $200k as an upper bound.
- WHO (legacy macro guidance): 1× and 3× GDP per capita; widely criticised for low-income settings and largely deprecated.
- Australia (PBAC) / Canada (CADTH): no fixed threshold; pragmatic interpretation around AUD/CAD 50k-100k/QALY.

## Interpretation

A threshold is not an algorithmic decision rule — it is a guidepost. Modifiers (disease severity, end-of-life, equity, budget impact, uncertainty) shift the effective threshold. The "shadow price" interpretation says the threshold should equal the marginal opportunity cost in the system; estimates for the NHS suggest ≈£12,936/QALY (Claxton et al. 2015), below NICE's official band.

## Worked example (medicine)

An oncology biologic with ICER £45,000/QALY is above NICE's standard £30,000 ceiling but may be accepted under the end-of-life modifier (life-extending in incurable disease, <24 months remaining life expectancy, treatment extends life by ≥3 months).

## How to report

Always declare the WTP used, justify the choice (default body's threshold + jurisdiction), and present the CEAC across a range of WTPs so reviewers can see how robust the conclusion is.
