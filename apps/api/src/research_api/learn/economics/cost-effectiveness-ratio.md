## Definition

The cost-effectiveness ratio (CER) is the cost of an intervention divided by a single measure of clinical effect — life-years saved, fractures prevented, infections averted, symptom-free days, and so on. A CER summarises one strategy in isolation; on its own it cannot tell you whether the intervention is good value because there is no comparator.

## Formula

```
CER = Total cost / Total effect
```

## Interpretation

A CER of £8,000 per revision avoided means it takes £8,000 of healthcare spending to prevent one revision of the chosen procedure. Lower is better — but the number is meaningless without a comparison strategy and a willingness-to-pay anchor.

## Worked example (orthopaedics)

A unit costs £12 million per year to run its primary total knee arthroplasty service and avoids 1,500 future revisions over the lifetime horizon. The CER is £12,000,000 / 1,500 = £8,000 per revision avoided.

## How to report

Always pair a CER with the comparator that produced it; otherwise an ICER (see [incremental-cost-effectiveness-ratio](?slug=incremental-cost-effectiveness-ratio)) is what reviewers will actually expect. Report perspective, time horizon, discount rate, and currency year alongside the headline number.
